Lucy's Journey

Lucy was rescued in 2018 from a high kill shelter in Mississippi through the licensed Rescue "For The Love of Dogs, Vermont".
She was adopted by me and lives happily in New Hampshire now. Lucy is my second Great Pyrenees rescue.

About Lucy

Lucy

Lucy is a Great Pyrenees dog. The Great Pyrenees breed is a Livestock Guardian Dog (LGD) typically guarding sheep on a farm from hungry wolves.

Lucy was adopted through For the Love Of Dogs, Vermont in August of 2018. She was a rescue from Mississippi found in a high kill shelter. She was two years old at the time, and was terribly underweight. Having just weened her puppies, she was only 68 pounds. Close to six years later, she is currently 135 pounds living in Wakefield, New Hampshire with her Mom, Keira. Lucy loves the snow, which is why New Hampsire is the perfect place for this floof.

Barking and Lounging

Fosters help transition rescues

Consider fostering

All dogs aren't prepared or appropriate for shelter life. Often, dogs need some time to adjust, unwind and relax before they find their FURever home.

By registering as a Foster to rescue dogs, you are helping transition a frightened, often malnutritioned, sometimes underweight dog ease into family living.

The Foster's job is to introduce dogs to learning family living, what it's like to be just a dog. A family member. Fosters help dogs understand that all people aren't bad, but loving and friendly.

Foster parents are an invaluable part of the care and rehabilitation of young, sick, or special needs animals. Fosters must be 18 years or older, be willing and able to take your fosters to follow-up veterinary appointments every 2-3 weeks, keep your foster pets separate from household pets for two weeks or longer in a spare room or contained area. Feed, care, socialize and monitor the health of the foster animal(s). A securely fenced yard is required if fostering dogs.

Things to consider when adopting or becoming a foster

  • Meet the foster parents!
  • Avoid puppies if you're new to fostering.
  • Make sure you have adequate outdoor space.
  • Prepare for crying and little sleep on your foster dog's first night.
  • Introduce your foster to your family and other pets on neutral territory.
  • Be patient, especially during the first two weeks.
